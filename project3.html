Retro Multiplayer Game Console

A distributed embedded system featuring dual-screen output, 72-hour battery life, and custom mixed-signal hardware.

My Role: Lead Electrical Engineer

Leading a team of three Computer Engineers, I was solely responsible for the entire hardware lifecycle. This included the schematic capture and PCB layout of all three system boards (Main Console, Audio Subsystem, and Controllers) using Altium Designer. I engineered the power architecture to support 72-hour runtimes, designed the mixed-signal VGA output stages, and managed the physical fabrication of the enclosures. Additionally, I bridged the gap between hardware and software, writing the low-level drivers for the Bluetooth stack and audio synchronization.

Technical Specifications
  • MCU Architecture: Dual-Core ESP32-WROVER (Master) + 2x ATmega328P (Slaves)
  • Video Output: Direct Digital GPIO VGA (320x200 @ 60Hz)
  • Wireless: HC-05 Bluetooth Classic (UART @ 9600 baud)
  • Power System: 3.7V Parallel Li-Ion + 5V Boost Converter + UPS
  • Synchronization: Hardwired UART Bridge (115200 baud)

1. System Architecture & Design Strategy

The project required a robust, low-latency architecture capable of running real-time multiplayer games like Tetris, Pong, and Minesweeper. As the Lead Hardware Engineer, I made the critical architectural decision to implement a Distributed Embedded Network rather than a monolithic system. This approach isolated critical tasks—video generation, game logic, and audio processing—across specific hardware modules to prevent resource contention.

Core Subsystems:

  • Main Console (The Brain): Comprises two ESP32-WROVER units. I implemented a direct UART link (GPIO 16/17) between them to synchronize game states with zero latency, bypassing the unpredictable overhead of Wi-Fi.
  • Wireless Controllers (The Input): Custom-designed handheld units powered by ATmega328P microcontrollers. These sample analog joystick data and transmit packetized inputs via HC-05 Bluetooth modules.
  • Audio Subsystem (The Sound): A dedicated PCB hosting a DFPlayer Mini and an ATmega328P. I designed this to be triggered via GPIO interrupts, completely offloading audio processing from the main game loop to prevent VGA signal jitter.

2. Detailed Hardware Implementation

My primary responsibility was the design and fabrication of the system's hardware. This involved moving from proof-of-concept breadboards to three custom-fabricated Printed Circuit Boards (PCBs).

Power Architecture Design

I engineered a power system designed for endurance and stability. The system uses two 18650 Lithium-Ion batteries (3300mAh each) wired in parallel, providing a total capacity of 6.6Ah at 3.7V.

  • Regulation: I integrated a high-efficiency DC-DC Step-Up Boost Converter to elevate the 3.7V battery voltage to a stable 5V, required for the ATmega328P and HC-05 modules.
  • UPS Functionality: The design includes a load-sharing circuit that allows the device to charge via USB-C while simultaneously powering the system, ensuring uninterrupted gameplay.
  • Efficiency Analysis: Calculated usable energy is approximately 22.47 Wh (after 92% conversion efficiency), enabling extended runtimes.
Signal Integrity & Mixed-Signal Design

Generating a clean VGA signal from a microcontroller requires precise signal integrity.

  • Logic Level Shifting: The HC-05 Bluetooth module operates at 3.3V logic, while the ATmega328P runs at 5V. I designed voltage divider circuits (1kΩ / 2.2kΩ) on the PCB TX/RX lines to safely step down signals and prevent overvoltage damage.
  • Audio Isolation: To prevent digital switching noise from coupling into the audio output, I physically separated the Audio Subsystem onto its own PCB with a dedicated ground plane.
  • VGA Output: I routed the 5-pin VGA signals (Red, Green, Blue, H-Sync, V-Sync) directly from the ESP32 GPIOs, carefully matching trace lengths to minimize skew.
